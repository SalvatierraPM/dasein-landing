# How Many Signals Should a Strategic Foresight Dossier Deliver?

**Research Date:** 2026-03-27
**Purpose:** Inform Dasein Foresight's signal methodology for client deliverables
**Confidence:** MEDIUM-HIGH (strong convergence across multiple sources, though few firms publish exact numbers)

---

## Executive Summary

A strategic foresight dossier should deliver **15-25 curated signals**, organized into **3 tiers** and **clustered by theme**. This range sits at the intersection of cognitive science (executives can process 3-5 chunks in working memory), practitioner consensus (6-12 per radar sector, 2-3 scenarios requiring 5-8 signals each), and consulting firm patterns (5-6 macro trends decomposed into 3-4 signals each). Below 12, you lack the diversity to build credible scenarios. Above 30, you trigger signal fatigue and decision paralysis.

## 2. Academic Frameworks

### Cognitive Load Theory (The Hard Ceiling)

The most important constraint is not methodological -- it is neurological.

- **Miller's Law (1956):** Working memory holds 7 +/- 2 items. Originally from George Miller's landmark paper.
- **Modern revision (Cowan, 2001+):** Working memory capacity is now understood as **4 +/- 1 distinct items**. This is a significant downward revision from Miller.
- **Chunking effect:** Experts overcome limits by recognizing patterns as single units. A novice processes C-A-T as 3 items; an expert processes "cat" as 1 chunk.
- **Under cognitive load:** Decision-makers exhibit attentional narrowing, shift to simple heuristics, and cannot simultaneously maintain and manipulate multiple variables.

**Implication for signal dossiers:** Executives can hold ~4 macro-themes in active working memory. Each theme can contain ~4-5 supporting signals (chunked as a coherent narrative). This yields a natural ceiling of **~20 signals** before processing degrades -- and a sweet spot of **15-18** for comfortable comprehension.

**Source:** Global Council for Behavioral Science, gc-bs.org/articles/the-impact-of-cognitive-load-on-decision-making-efficiency/

### Weak Signals Theory (Ansoff & Hiltunen)

- **Ansoff (1975):** Defined weak signals as "ambiguous indications of forthcoming impactful disruption." His framework was about detecting strategic surprises before they fully materialize. Ansoff did not prescribe a specific number -- his concern was that organizations filter *too aggressively* and miss critical signals.
- **Hiltunen (2008):** Refined weak signals along three dimensions: **novelty, ambiguity, and interpretive distance**. Higher scores on all three = weaker signal = harder to detect but potentially more important.
- **Ilmola & Kuusi (2006):** Warned that "perceptual filters that decreased the number of signals processed" can paradoxically hinder foresight. Over-filtering is as dangerous as under-filtering.

**Implication:** The literature does NOT provide an optimal number. Instead, it establishes a tension: filter enough to avoid noise, but not so much that you eliminate genuine weak signals. The solution is **tiering** (see Section 3), not reducing.

### Signal-to-Noise in Strategic Intelligence

No single academic paper prescribes "deliver exactly N signals." The field converges on process-based guidance:

1. **Scan broadly** (100s of signals)
2. **Filter by relevance, credibility, and novelty** (reduce by ~80%)
3. **Prioritize by impact and uncertainty** (reduce by another ~50%)
4. **Cluster into themes** that create coherent narratives
5. **Deliver the clusters** with their supporting signals

The NCBI horizon scanning review confirms this funnel: one real-world engagement scanned 70 signals and prioritized 20. Futures Platform recommends 6-12 per focused radar exercise. The convergence point across sources is **15-25 signals in the final deliverable**.

**Source:** NCBI, ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK556423/

## 3. Signal Categorization: How to Structure the Dossier

### Recommended Tiering System (3 Tiers)

Based on the research, signals should be organized along **three simultaneous dimensions**:

#### Tier by Signal Type (Certainty Gradient)

| Type | Definition | Expected Count | Role in Dossier |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Confirmed Trends** | Established, data-backed trajectories | 5-8 | Anchor the analysis; provide credibility |
| **Emerging Signals** | Early evidence of change, direction unclear | 5-10 | The actionable core; where strategic advantage lives |
| **Weak Signals / Wild Cards** | Speculative, high-ambiguity, high-potential-impact | 3-5 | Stretch thinking; prevent blind spots |

This maps directly to the Johari Window framework used by Futures Platform:
- Confirmed trends = Known-Knowns
- Emerging signals = Known-Unknowns
- Weak signals = Unknown-Knowns
- Wild cards = Unknown-Unknowns

**Source:** Futures Platform, futuresplatform.com/blog/four-types-futures-intelligence-johari-window

#### Tier by Time Horizon

| Horizon | Timeframe | Signal Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Near** | 0-2 years | Mostly confirmed trends and fast-moving emerging signals |
| **Mid** | 2-5 years | Mix of emerging signals and some weak signals gaining strength |
| **Far** | 5-10+ years | Primarily weak signals and wild cards |

This aligns with the NCBI horizon scanning guidance which recommends: near-term (5 years), medium (5-10 years), and long-term (10+ years).

#### Tier by Domain (STEEP/PESTLE)

| Domain | Description |
|---|---|
| **S** - Social/Societal | Demographics, culture, behavior, values |
| **T** - Technological | Innovation, infrastructure, digital transformation |
| **E** - Economic | Markets, trade, business models, labor |
| **E** - Environmental | Climate, resources, sustainability, biodiversity |
| **P** - Political/Policy | Regulation, governance, geopolitics |
| **L** - Legal (optional) | Compliance, litigation, IP, standards |

Futures Platform uses PESTE (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental) as their standard framework, recommending ~10 phenomena per sector for a comprehensive radar.

**Source:** Futures Platform horizon scanning guide, futuresplatform.com/blog/how-to-horizon-scanning-guideline

## 4. Signal Metadata: What Each Signal Should Carry

Based on synthesis of signal card formats (Service Design Tools), horizon scanning best practices (NCBI), and foresight radar methodology (Futures Platform):

### Required Metadata per Signal

```
SIGNAL CARD TEMPLATE
====================

1. Signal ID          [unique identifier, e.g., SIG-2026-017]
2. Title              [catching headline, ~10 words max]
3. Summary            [2-3 sentence narrative of what's happening]
4. Evidence           [specific data point, event, or source proving this is real]
5. Source URL          [primary source link]
6. Source Date         [when the evidence was published/observed]
7. Signal Type        [Confirmed Trend | Emerging Signal | Weak Signal | Wild Card]
8. Time Horizon       [Near: 0-2yr | Mid: 2-5yr | Far: 5-10yr]
9. STEEP Domain       [Social | Tech | Economic | Environmental | Political]
10. Impact Level      [High | Medium | Low -- on the client's specific context]
11. Certainty         [High | Medium | Low -- likelihood of materialization]
12. Velocity          [Accelerating | Steady | Decelerating | Unknown]
13. Implications      [2-3 bullet points: "So what?" for the client]
14. Connected Signals [IDs of related signals in this dossier]
15. Scenario Link     [which scenario(s) this signal feeds into]
```

### Optional Enrichment Fields

```
16. Sector Relevance  [specific industry/vertical impact]
17. Geographic Scope  [global | regional | local]
18. Contrarian View   [strongest argument this signal is noise]
19. Action Trigger    [what event would confirm/deny this signal]
20. Visual/Image      [supporting imagery or data visualization]
```

**Sources:** Service Design Tools signal cards template (servicedesigntools.org/tools/signal-cards); NCBI horizon scanning prioritization criteria: credibility, novelty, likelihood, impact, relevance, time to awareness, time to prepare (ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK556423/)

## 5. How Signals Connect to Scenarios

### The Funnel: Signals -> Clusters -> Driving Forces -> Scenarios

```
200+ raw signals (scanning phase)
    |
    v  [filter by relevance, credibility, novelty]
40-50 relevant signals
    |
    v  [prioritize by impact x uncertainty]
15-25 curated signals (THE DOSSIER)
    |
    v  [cluster by affinity]
4-6 signal clusters / themes
    |
    v  [identify 2 critical uncertainties from clusters]
2 axes of uncertainty
    |
    v  [2x2 matrix]
3-4 scenarios (typically 2-3 are most useful)
    |
    v  [stress test strategy against each]
Strategic options + recommended actions
```

### How Many Signals Per Scenario?

- Each scenario should draw on **5-8 signals** that, combined, create a coherent narrative
- Signals can (and should) appear in multiple scenarios -- the same signal plays out differently depending on context
- The classic Shell/GBN method generates **7-9 mini-scenarios** before consolidating to **2-3 final scenarios**
- Standard practice is **2-3 scenarios** per engagement (Wikipedia/scenario planning literature; Shell methodology)

### Minimum Viable Signal Set for Credible Scenarios

To build 3 credible scenarios, you need:
- At least **2 distinct dimensions of uncertainty** (the 2 axes)
- Each axis anchored by **3-4 signals** showing the range of possible outcomes
- Plus **3-5 invariant signals** (confirmed trends present in ALL scenarios)
- **Minimum: ~12 signals.** Below this, scenarios feel thin and arbitrary.
- **Sweet spot: 15-20.** Enough diversity for rich narratives without cognitive overload.

**Source:** Wikipedia/Scenario planning (Shell produces 7-9 mini-scenarios, consolidates to 2-3; en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scenario_planning)

## 6. Avoiding Signal Fatigue: Practical Design Principles

### The Problem
Signal fatigue occurs when decision-makers receive more signals than they can meaningfully process, leading to:
- **Analysis paralysis** (too many inputs, no clear priority)
- **Flat hierarchy** (everything feels equally important = nothing is important)
- **Disengagement** ("I'll read this later" = never)

### Design Principles to Prevent Fatigue

1. **Lead with the "So What"**: Every signal must answer "why should I care about this?" in the first sentence. Implications first, evidence second.

2. **3-tier visual hierarchy**: Make the 5-8 most critical signals visually dominant. Supporting signals should be accessible but secondary. Weak signals/wild cards should be clearly marked as speculative.

3. **Cluster, don't list**: A flat list of 20 signals is overwhelming. 4-5 clusters of 4-5 signals each is digestible. The clusters ARE the story.

4. **Progressive disclosure**: Executive summary (5 key themes, one page) -> Signal overview (all 15-25 signals, 2-3 pages) -> Deep dives (full signal cards, appendix). Let the reader choose their depth.

5. **Signal radar visualization**: Place signals on a radar plot with axes for time horizon (distance from center) and certainty (color). This makes the entire dossier graspable in one glance.

6. **One page per signal maximum**: Signal cards should be scannable in 30 seconds. The constraint forces clarity.

7. **Always include "action triggers"**: For each signal, specify: "If [event X] happens, this signal is confirmed and you should [action Y]." This transforms passive intelligence into active monitoring.

## 7. Recommended Signal Count for Dasein Foresight Dossiers

### The Recommendation: 18-24 Signals per Dossier

| Component | Count | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Confirmed Trends** | 5-7 | Anchor the analysis; high confidence; align with client's known landscape |
| **Emerging Signals** | 8-12 | The strategic core; where insight lives; enough diversity for 3 scenarios |
| **Weak Signals** | 3-4 | Stretch thinking; at least 1 per STEEP domain |
| **Wild Cards** | 1-2 | "What if?" provocations; prevent tunnel vision |
| **TOTAL** | 18-24 | |

### Organized As:

- **4-5 thematic clusters** (each containing 4-5 signals from mixed types)
- Each cluster maps to **at least 1 STEEP domain** (but clusters can span domains)
- Each cluster feeds into **at least 1 scenario**
- Clusters ordered by **strategic urgency** (near-term actionable first)

### Deliverable Format:

1. **Page 1: Signal Radar** -- all signals plotted on a single visual (time horizon x certainty, color-coded by domain)
2. **Pages 2-3: Executive Narrative** -- 4-5 cluster summaries, 1 paragraph each, with "so what" implications
3. **Pages 4-8: Signal Cards** -- one card per signal, following the metadata template above
4. **Pages 9-10: Scenario Sketches** -- 3 scenarios built from signal clusters, with probability estimates
5. **Page 11: Action Matrix** -- signals mapped to strategic options, with decision triggers and timelines

### Why This Range:

- **Below 15:** Insufficient diversity to build credible scenarios; risks cherry-picking signals that confirm existing beliefs
- **Above 25:** Triggers cognitive overload (exceeds 4-5 chunks x 5 items/chunk); signals start competing for attention rather than building a narrative
- **18-24 sweet spot:** Aligns with Futures Platform's ~10/sector guidance (applied to 2-3 focal sectors), NCBI's 70->20 filter ratio, Deloitte's 5 trends x 4 sub-signals pattern, and cognitive science's 4+/-1 chunk limit
